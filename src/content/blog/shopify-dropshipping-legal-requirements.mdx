Dropshipping on Shopify is a legitimate business model, but it carries legal obligations that many new merchants overlook. You are the seller of record for every transaction, which means you bear full legal responsibility for product quality, accurate advertising, tax collection, customs compliance, and consumer protection. The fact that a supplier manufactures and ships the product does not transfer any of these obligations away from you.

This guide covers every legal requirement that Shopify dropshippers must meet to operate lawfully and protect their business.

---

## What Business Registration Do You Need for Dropshipping?

Operating a dropshipping business without proper registration exposes you to personal liability, tax penalties, and potential criminal charges for unlicensed business activity.

**Required registrations and licenses:**

| Registration | Purpose | Where to File | Typical Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Business entity (LLC or Corporation) | Separates personal and business liability | State Secretary of State | $50-$500 |
| EIN (Employer Identification Number) | Tax identification for your business | IRS (online, free) | Free |
| State business license | Legal authorization to operate | State/county licensing office | $25-$400 |
| Sales tax permit | Authorization to collect and remit sales tax | State Department of Revenue | Free-$100 |
| DBA (Doing Business As) | If operating under a name different from your LLC | County clerk's office | $10-$100 |
| Home occupation permit | If operating from a residential address | City/county zoning office | $0-$150 |
| Seller's permit | Required in some states for wholesale purchasing | State tax authority | Free-$50 |

**Choosing your business entity:**

An LLC is the most common and recommended entity for Shopify dropshippers. It provides personal asset protection, tax flexibility (choose to be taxed as a sole proprietor, partnership, S-corp, or C-corp), and relatively low maintenance requirements.

A sole proprietorship requires no formal filing but provides zero personal liability protection. If a customer sues your dropshipping business and wins, they can go after your personal bank accounts, home, and other assets.

Form your LLC in the state where you live and operate, not in Delaware or Wyoming for perceived tax benefits. The cost of maintaining a registered agent and foreign qualification in your home state typically exceeds any savings.

## How Do Sales Tax and Customs Work for Dropshippers?

Sales tax obligations for dropshippers follow the same economic nexus rules as any other Shopify merchant, but the logistics are more complex when products ship from suppliers in different states or countries.

**Domestic dropshipping (US-based suppliers):**
- You must collect sales tax in states where you have economic nexus
- Your supplier may also have nexus in states where they ship from, but that does not affect your obligation
- If your supplier collects and remits tax on your behalf (rare), you need documentation to avoid double collection
- Register for sales tax permits in all nexus states before collecting

**International dropshipping (typically from China):**
- You still must collect US sales tax from your customers based on your nexus
- Products under $800 in declared value enter the US duty-free under the de minimis exemption
- Products over $800 may incur customs duties, which you should factor into pricing
- Section 321 de minimis shipments are under increased regulatory scrutiny as of 2026
- Certain product categories (textiles, electronics, food) have specific import requirements regardless of value

**Common dropshipping tax mistakes:**
1. Not collecting sales tax because "the supplier handles it" (they almost never do)
2. Failing to register for sales tax permits before collecting
3. Not accounting for customs duties in international product pricing
4. Ignoring economic nexus thresholds in states where you have high sales volume

## What Consumer Protection Laws Apply to Dropshipping?

As the retailer, you are bound by federal and state consumer protection regulations. These laws do not care whether you manufactured, warehoused, or even touched the product.

**FTC Mail Order Rule (16 CFR Part 435):**
- You must ship products within the timeframe stated on your site
- If no shipping time is stated, you must ship within 30 days of receiving payment
- If you cannot meet the shipping deadline, you must notify the customer and offer the option to cancel for a full refund
- This is critical for dropshippers with long shipping times from overseas suppliers

**Practical implications:** If your suppliers ship from China with 15-30 day delivery times, you must clearly disclose this before the customer completes the purchase. Advertising "free shipping" without disclosing 3-4 week delivery times violates the FTC's prohibition on deceptive advertising.

**State consumer protection laws:**
- Most states have Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices (UDAP) statutes
- These laws provide additional consumer protections beyond federal requirements
- State attorneys general can bring enforcement actions against deceptive e-commerce practices
- Customers can file private lawsuits under many state consumer protection statutes

**Product safety requirements:**
- All products sold in the US must comply with applicable safety standards
- Children's products must meet CPSIA requirements including lead content limits, phthalate restrictions, and mandatory third-party testing
- Electronics must meet FCC requirements for electromagnetic interference
- Textiles must comply with CPSC flammability standards
- You are responsible for ensuring compliance even if your supplier claims the products meet standards

## What Should Your Supplier Agreement Include?

A written supplier agreement is essential for legal protection. Verbal agreements and informal arrangements leave you exposed when problems arise.

**Critical clauses for dropshipping supplier agreements:**

1. **Product quality standards** — Define acceptable quality levels, defect rates, and testing requirements
2. **Product compliance certifications** — Require suppliers to provide documentation that products meet US safety standards
3. **Indemnification** — The supplier agrees to defend and compensate you for claims arising from product defects or safety violations
4. **Insurance requirements** — Require suppliers to maintain product liability insurance with minimum coverage limits
5. **Shipping timeframes** — Define maximum processing and shipping times with penalties for delays
6. **Return and refund procedures** — Establish who bears the cost of returns and how defective products are handled
7. **Intellectual property warranties** — The supplier warrants that products do not infringe any third-party trademarks, patents, or copyrights
8. **Confidentiality** — Protect your customer data and business information
9. **Termination provisions** — Define conditions under which either party can end the relationship
10. **Governing law and dispute resolution** — Specify which jurisdiction's laws apply (this is especially important for international suppliers)

**Reality check on enforcement:** Supplier agreements with overseas manufacturers are difficult to enforce across international borders. The agreement provides a paper trail and demonstrates due diligence, but practical enforcement may require litigation in the supplier's home country. This is why product liability insurance for your own business is non-negotiable.

## What Advertising Rules Apply to Dropshipping?

Dropshippers frequently face FTC scrutiny for deceptive advertising practices. The temptation to embellish product claims is high when you are selling products you have never used.

**FTC truth-in-advertising requirements:**
- All product claims must be truthful and substantiated
- You cannot claim a product has benefits without competent and reliable evidence
- Testimonials must reflect honest opinions and typical results
- "Before and after" claims need documentation
- Country of origin must be accurately stated
- Price comparisons (was/now) must reference genuine former prices

**Common dropshipping advertising violations:**
1. Using supplier product images that show higher quality than the actual product
2. Claiming products are "US-made" or "shipped from the US" when they ship from China
3. Inflating retail prices to create fake discount percentages
4. Using fake reviews or testimonials
5. Claiming health or safety benefits without scientific evidence
6. Not disclosing material facts about the product (materials, sizing, country of origin)

**Social media advertising specifics:**
- Sponsored content and paid partnerships must be clearly disclosed
- Influencer endorsements must include #ad or #sponsored
- Facebook, Instagram, and TikTok ad policies add additional restrictions beyond FTC rules
- Retargeting ads must comply with GDPR consent requirements for EU users

## How Do You Handle Returns and Refunds in Dropshipping?

Your return and refund policy must comply with consumer protection laws regardless of your supplier's willingness to accept returns.

**Legal requirements:**
- Display your return policy conspicuously before purchase
- If you do not post a return policy, some states default to requiring a full refund within 30 days
- Credit card chargeback rights give customers 120 days to dispute charges regardless of your return policy
- Defective products must be refunded or replaced under implied warranty laws

**Practical dropshipping return strategies:**
1. **Refund without return** for items under $20-30 (cheaper than paying return shipping to China)
2. **Partial refund** for items that do not match the listing but are still usable
3. **Domestic return address** if you have any US location that can receive returns
4. **Replacement shipping** from the supplier for defective items rather than processing a return

Budget 5-15% of revenue for returns and refunds. Dropshipping return rates tend to be higher than traditional e-commerce because customers receive products they have never physically evaluated and shipping times create more opportunities for buyer's remorse.

## What Steps Should You Take This Week?

**Day 1-2: Establish your legal foundation.**
- Form an LLC in your home state
- Apply for an EIN from the IRS (free, online, immediate)
- Obtain a state business license
- Open a business bank account separate from personal finances

**Day 3-4: Handle tax obligations.**
- Determine which states you have economic nexus in
- Register for sales tax permits in nexus states
- Configure Shopify Tax to collect in registered states
- Understand customs obligations for your product categories

**Day 5: Secure supplier agreements.**
- Draft or request a formal supplier agreement covering the clauses listed above
- Request product compliance certifications from all suppliers
- Request certificates of insurance from suppliers
- Order samples of all products you sell to verify quality

**Day 6-7: Review compliance.**
- Audit all product listings for truthful advertising
- Ensure shipping times are accurately disclosed
- Post a compliant return and refund policy
- Verify your privacy policy covers your actual data practices

**Ongoing:**
- Collect and remit sales tax on schedule in all registered states
- Monitor FTC enforcement actions in e-commerce for emerging compliance issues
- Update supplier agreements annually
- Maintain product liability insurance
- Respond to customer complaints and refund requests within legal timeframes

Dropshipping is a legal and viable business model, but it requires the same legal infrastructure as any other retail operation.
