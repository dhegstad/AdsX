The About page is the most visited non-product page on almost every Shopify store—and the most consistently underperforming. Merchants invest heavily in product photography, ad copy, and homepage design, then throw together an About page with a paragraph of generic brand language and no strategic intent.

This matters because the About page is where trust is built. Data across thousands of e-commerce stores shows that visitors who view the About page convert at 1.5-2x the rate of those who do not. They are not visiting the About page to admire your prose—they are trying to answer a fundamental question: "Can I trust these people with my money?"

This guide shows you how to write a [Shopify](https://shopify.pxf.io/c/6318547/3797171/13624) About page that answers that question convincingly and moves visitors toward purchase.

## Why Do Visitors Go to the About Page Before Buying?

When a visitor clicks your About page, they are signaling a specific psychological need: trust verification. They have seen your products, they may be interested, but they have not yet committed. The About page visit is the evaluation step between interest and purchase.

This behavior is especially common for DTC brands where visitors have no prior relationship with the company. Unlike buying from Amazon or a department store, purchasing from an independent Shopify store requires trusting an unfamiliar business. The About page is where that trust is either built or broken.

Three psychological needs drive About page visits:

**Legitimacy assessment.** "Is this a real business?" Visitors look for evidence of real people, a physical presence, and operational history. Stock photos and vague corporate language fail this test. Real team photos and specific origin details pass it.

**Value alignment.** "Do these people share my values?" Modern consumers, especially younger demographics, choose brands that reflect their personal beliefs around sustainability, quality, community, or innovation. The About page is where you make that alignment explicit.

**Emotional connection.** "Do I feel good about buying from these people?" Humans prefer buying from people they like. A compelling origin story, authentic team personalities, and genuine brand voice create the emotional connection that tips purchase decisions.

## How Do You Write a Brand Story That Sells?

The brand origin story is the emotional core of your About page. It transforms your store from an anonymous website into a business run by real people with real motivations. The most effective brand stories follow a predictable narrative structure.

**Start with the problem.** Every compelling brand story begins with frustration, dissatisfaction, or a gap in the market that the founder experienced personally. "I spent three years searching for running shoes that didn't destroy my knees" is more compelling than "We are passionate about athletic footwear."

**Introduce the person, not the brand.** The story should center on the founder or founding team as people. Use first-person perspective. Share specific details—where you were, what you were doing, what triggered the idea. Specificity creates authenticity that generic brand language cannot replicate.

**Describe the turning point.** What moment transformed frustration into action? This is the narrative hinge that makes your story memorable. "I was sitting in a physical therapy office for the third time that year when I realized no one was solving this problem" is a specific, relatable moment that sticks.

**Connect the origin to the present.** Bridge from the founding moment to what your brand is today. "That frustration led me to spend two years working with biomechanists to design [product], and today we've helped 40,000 runners stay pain-free." This arc gives the story resolution and credibility.

**Keep it honest.** Consumers can detect manufactured authenticity. If your brand started as a side project in a garage, say that. If you started with a loan from your parents, say that. Vulnerability and honesty build more trust than polished corporate narratives.

## How Should Team Photos and Bios Be Presented?

Team presence on the About page answers the legitimacy question directly. Visitors want to see that real humans stand behind the products they are considering buying.

**Use real, professional photographs.** This does not mean stiff corporate headshots. Candid or semi-candid photos of team members in your workspace, with your products, or in environments related to your brand feel authentic and approachable. Avoid stock photos entirely—visitors recognize them and trust drops immediately.

**Include the founder prominently.** The founder photo and bio should be the most prominent team element. For DTC brands, the founder is the brand's human face. A brief bio that connects the founder's background to the brand's mission reinforces the origin story.

**Show the team at work if possible.** Photos of your team in a warehouse packing orders, in a workshop making products, or in a meeting discussing designs show operational reality. These behind-the-scenes glimpses build trust that posed portraits cannot.

**Keep bios brief and human.** Two to three sentences per team member: role, background, and a personal detail. "Sarah runs our customer experience team. She spent 8 years in hospitality before joining us, and she's the reason our customers rave about our support. When she's not solving problems, she's training for her next half-marathon." This format is professional, warm, and memorable.

## Where Does the Mission Statement Fit?

A mission or values statement communicates what your brand stands for beyond selling products. For modern consumers, brand values are a meaningful purchase factor.

**State your mission in one sentence.** "We make sustainable activewear that performs as well as conventional alternatives" is clear and specific. Long, abstract mission statements ("We strive to empower individuals through innovative solutions that transform the paradigm of...") communicate nothing.

**Support the mission with evidence.** If you claim sustainability, show your certifications, material sourcing, or carbon offset data. If you claim quality, reference your testing process or manufacturing standards. Unsupported claims feel hollow.

**Present values as 3-5 specific commitments.** Rather than listing abstract values ("Integrity, Innovation, Excellence"), make them concrete and specific: "Every product is tested for 10,000 uses before it ships." "We donate 1% of revenue to ocean cleanup." "We pay our factory workers 2x the local average wage." Specific commitments are believable; abstract values are not.

**Connect values to customer benefit.** "We source organic cotton because it's better for the planet—and softer on your skin" links your values to a tangible benefit the customer receives. This makes your mission relevant rather than self-congratulatory.

## How Do You Add Social Proof to the About Page?

Social proof on the About page reinforces the trust that your brand story builds. It shifts the credibility source from your own claims to third-party validation.

**Include press mentions with logos and quotes.** If your brand has been featured in publications, show the logos and pull one-line quotes: "The best running shoe we've tested this year — Runner's World." Press mentions transfer credibility from established media to your brand.

**Display milestone numbers.** "50,000+ customers served," "4.8 average star rating," "Shipped to 45 countries" — these numbers quantify your brand's impact and scale. Place them in a visually distinct section with large typography or icon-based callouts.

**Add customer testimonials.** Two to three testimonials with photos, names, and specific results provide persuasive proof on the About page. Choose testimonials that align with the narrative of your brand story.

**Mention partnerships or certifications.** B Corp certification, organic certifications, fair trade partnerships, or industry awards all serve as third-party validation. Display their badges or logos in a compact section.

## What CTAs Should the About Page Include?

An About page without CTAs is a conversion dead end. Visitors who have read your story and are emotionally engaged need a clear path to the next step.

**Mid-page CTA after the origin story.** Once visitors have read your brand story and feel connected, offer a natural next step: "See what we've built" with a link to your flagship collection, or "Try our bestseller" with a link to your most popular product.

**End-of-page CTA as a final conversion point.** After team photos, values, and social proof, close with a strong CTA: "Ready to see what the buzz is about? Browse the Collection" or "Join 50,000+ happy customers — Shop Now."

**Newsletter signup as a soft CTA.** Not every About page visitor is ready to buy. A newsletter signup with a value proposition ("Get 15% off your first order + weekly style tips") captures email addresses from visitors who need more time.

**Match CTA design to the rest of your store.** Use the same button styles, colors, and design patterns from your product pages. Consistency reinforces the browsing-to-buying transition.

## How Do You Structure and Implement Your About Page?

Follow this step-by-step process:

1. **Draft your brand origin story.** Write the problem, the person, the turning point, and the present-day resolution in 200-400 words. Use first person and specific details.
2. **Gather team photos.** Schedule a photo session or collect candid workplace photos. Ensure quality is professional but not overly staged.
3. **Write your mission statement.** Condense your brand purpose into one clear sentence. Support it with 3-5 specific, evidence-backed value commitments.
4. **Collect social proof assets.** Pull press quotes, review data, milestone numbers, and certifications. Create visual assets for each.
5. **Write 2-3 customer testimonials.** Select testimonials that reinforce your brand narrative and address common objections.
6. **Design the page layout.** Use your Shopify theme's page builder to create sections: hero image, origin story, team grid, mission/values, social proof, and CTA blocks.
7. **Add mid-page and end-page CTAs.** Link to your flagship collection or bestselling products. Use compelling, action-oriented button text.
8. **Add a newsletter signup.** Include a value-driven email capture form near the bottom of the page.
9. **Optimize for mobile.** Stack all sections vertically, ensure team photos are visible and clear, and make CTAs full-width.
10. **Link the About page prominently.** Add it to your main navigation or footer. The page cannot build trust if visitors cannot find it.

Your Shopify About page is not a vanity page—it is a trust-building machine that directly influences conversion rates. Visitors who read your origin story, see your team, understand your values, and encounter your social proof are psychologically primed to purchase. By treating the About page as a strategic conversion asset rather than an afterthought, you capture the trust and emotional connection that turns browsers into loyal customers.
