There are roughly 21,500 to 24,000 public apps in the Shopify App Store as of mid-2026, depending on the tracker. About 7% carry the Built for Shopify badge, roughly 65% offer a free plan, and developers keep 100% of their first $1M in lifetime revenue. Below, every number with its source, and hedged where sources disagree.

![Charts and analytics data displayed on a laptop screen](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1460925895917-afdab827c52f?w=1200&q=80)

A note on method before the numbers: Shopify publishes no official real-time census of its App Store, so everything here comes from third-party trackers that crawl the store, plus Shopify's own documentation where policy numbers are concerned. Trackers disagree with each other, sometimes by thousands of apps, because they detect delistings and new launches differently. Where they diverge, this post shows the range rather than picking a winner. Figures were checked in July 2026 and will drift; the sources are linked so you can pull current numbers.

| Statistic | Figure | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Public apps | ~21,500 to ~24,000 | AppstorePulse (May 2026), AppNavigator (Jul 2026) |

Each row is unpacked below with links, caveats, and the disagreements between sources.

## How many Shopify apps are there?

| Source | Count | As of |
|---|---|---|
| [AppstorePulse](https://www.appstorepulse.com/reports/state-of-shopify-app-store-may-2026) | 21,509 live apps | May 31, 2026 |
| [AppNavigator](https://appnavigator.io/statistics/) | 23,765 listed apps | July 20, 2026 |

The two trackers roughly agree on trajectory even where they disagree on level. [AppNavigator](https://appnavigator.io/statistics/) reports about an 87% year-over-year increase and 1,730 apps added in the 30 days before July 20, 2026. [AppstorePulse](https://www.appstorepulse.com/reports/state-of-shopify-app-store-may-2026) counted 2,713 new launches in May 2026 against just 56 removals.

Two things stand out in those numbers. First, the growth rate is extraordinary by historical standards; the store sat in the high single-digit thousands for years before this acceleration, and AI-assisted development is the obvious explanation for why shipping an app got cheaper. Second, the launch-to-removal ratio (roughly 48 to 1 in May) means the catalog is inflating faster than it is being pruned, so raw app count says little about how many apps are actually maintained, installed, and alive in any commercial sense.

Supply is also more concentrated than the headline count suggests. Portfolio publishers ship dozens of small single-purpose apps under one roof; AppstorePulse identifies the largest portfolio, UnReact Inc., at 151 apps. Divide the catalog by unique developers rather than listings and the ecosystem looks meaningfully smaller, with earlier tracker estimates putting the developer base in the low five figures against twenty-plus thousand apps.

The concentration follows the money. Every top-ten category maps to a direct revenue lever (conversion, shipping cost, average order value, traffic), which is exactly why those categories are saturated: a new analytics app enters a field of nearly a thousand incumbents. Developers hunting for openings tend to do better in operational niches where a few dozen apps serve merchants with a specific, painful workflow; there is a reason lists of [what to build on Shopify's catalog APIs](/blog/things-to-build-shopify-product-catalog-api) skew toward unglamorous data plumbing rather than another upsell widget.

## Built for Shopify: the quality tier

The Built for Shopify badge marks apps meeting [Shopify's published quality, performance, and design standards](https://shopify.dev/docs/apps/launch/built-for-shopify):

- **1,497 badged apps** as of May 2026 per [AppstorePulse](https://www.appstorepulse.com/reports/state-of-shopify-app-store-may-2026), about 7% of the live catalog
- **1,556 badged apps** as of July 2026 per [AppNavigator](https://appnavigator.io/statistics/)
- **Seven of the ten most-reviewed apps** carry the badge, per AppstorePulse

That last stat is the interesting one. The badge population is small, but it is heavily over-represented at the top of the store, though causation runs both ways: successful apps have the resources to meet certification, and certification improves placement. Either way, the badge is becoming a machine-readable quality signal at exactly the moment app discovery is shifting toward AI-assisted recommendation, a dynamic examined in [how Built for Shopify affects app discovery in the AI era](/blog/built-for-shopify-app-discovery-ai-era).

## Pricing: free plans dominate the front door

From [AppstorePulse's May 2026 data](https://www.appstorepulse.com/reports/state-of-shopify-app-store-may-2026):

| Pricing model | Apps | Share |
|---|---|---|
| At least one free plan | 14,071 | 65% |
| Paid-only | 7,438 | 35% |
| Average pricing plans per app | 2.1 | n/a |

The freemium skew is rational: reviews drive App Store ranking, installs drive reviews, and free plans drive installs. The typical successful structure is a genuinely useful free tier with paid tiers gating volume or advanced features. Note what this stat does not say: it counts apps offering a free plan, not merchants paying nothing. No public tracker measures paid conversion across the store, and developers' anecdotal free-to-paid rates vary too widely to summarize honestly.

## The localization gap

One of the more actionable findings in the [AppstorePulse data](https://www.appstorepulse.com/reports/state-of-shopify-app-store-may-2026) gets little attention: **76.6% of live apps ship in English only** (16,470 apps), while just 9.3% support two to five languages and 7.2% support more than five. Shopify's merchant base is heavily international and growing fastest outside English-speaking markets, yet three quarters of the tools on offer never translated their listing or interface.

For developers, that is a rare structural opening in a crowded store: localizing an app and its listing into German, French, Japanese, or Portuguese puts you in a dramatically thinner competitive field than the English-language rankings for the same category. For merchants operating in non-English markets, it explains a familiar frustration, and it makes the apps that did localize easy to shortlist.

## Review dynamics

Reviews are the store's ranking currency, and the aggregate numbers (again per [AppstorePulse](https://www.appstorepulse.com/reports/state-of-shopify-app-store-may-2026), May 2026) look like this:

- **828,076 reviews** across currently live apps, out of 950,996 collected historically since 2013
- **4.65 stars** average rating across the catalog
- **21,254 new reviews** in May 2026, up 4.0% from April
- New May reviews averaged **4.81 stars**

The gap between the catalog average (4.65) and the average of fresh reviews (4.81) suggests ratings inflation is ongoing; developers have gotten systematically better at prompting happy merchants at the right moment. The practical consequence for merchants: a 4.8 rating no longer differentiates. Read the recent one-star and two-star reviews, where the actual failure modes live, and weight review volume and recency over the headline average. For developers, the same inflation means a sub-4.5 average is now a visible wound rather than a respectable score.

Review velocity is the underused signal in this data. Roughly 21,000 new reviews a month spread across 21,000+ apps means the median app collects close to zero; active review accumulation indicates an app that is installed, used, and supported right now, which a five-year-old 4.9 average does not. When two candidates look identical on rating, the one earning reviews this quarter is the safer install.

## The AI app wave

The clearest trend in the 2026 data is AI saturation among new launches, per [AppstorePulse](https://www.appstorepulse.com/reports/state-of-shopify-app-store-may-2026):

- **14.7% of all live apps** mention AI (3,163 of 21,509)
- **23.8% of May 2026 launches** mention AI (647 of 2,713), making AI apps about 1.6x over-represented among new arrivals relative to the existing catalog

Mentioning AI is not the same as being an AI product, and some of this is keyword opportunism. But the direction is unambiguous: roughly one in four new apps now positions on AI, spanning support agents, creative generation, catalog enrichment, and forecasting. The deeper shift is that AI is changing both sides of the market at once: it lowers the cost of building apps (visible in the launch counts above, and in how far [AI app builders](/blog/build-shopify-store-app-ai-replit-lovable-bolt-v0) have come) while changing how merchants and shoppers discover things, a trajectory that runs through [agentic commerce on Shopify](/blog/agentic-commerce-shopify-future).

## Developer economics: the revenue share

The policy numbers come straight from [Shopify's revenue share documentation](https://shopify.dev/docs/apps/launch/distribution/revenue-share):

| Term | Value |
|---|---|
| Registration for App Store distribution | One-time $19 USD |
| Revenue share on first $1M USD lifetime gross | 0% |
| Revenue share above $1M lifetime | 15% |
| Payment processing fee | 2.9%, charged separately |
| Large developers ($20M+ prior-year App Store earnings, or $100M+ gross company revenue) | 15% on all revenue |

The fine print that changed the economics: for earnings from January 1, 2025 onward, the $1M threshold is **lifetime**, not annual. Previously the counter [reset every year](https://betakit.com/shopify-app-developers-will-no-longer-be-exempt-from-sharing-their-first-1-million-usd-in-revenue-every-year/), which for a steady $1M/year app was the difference between paying Shopify nothing and paying 15% on most revenue at maturity. Revenue is aggregated across associated developer accounts, closing the multi-account workaround.

What no one can tell you: total developer earnings across the ecosystem. Shopify has cited multibillion-dollar cumulative partner economics in marketing materials over the years, but publishes no current per-app or aggregate App Store revenue data, so any "average app makes $X" claim you encounter is unverifiable. The observable proxies (rising launch rates, the badge race, and consolidation events like [Mantle shutting down](/blog/mantle-shutting-down-shopify-app-partners)) point to a market that is growing at the top while getting harder at the entry level.

## Install behavior: how many apps do merchants run?

The most repeated figure here, that the average store on [Shopify](https://shopify.pxf.io/c/6318547/3797171/13624) runs about six apps, is a commonly cited industry estimate (see [LaunchTip's discussion](https://www.launchtip.com/blog/how-many-apps-should-a-shopify-store-use) of the question) rather than an official Shopify statistic, and the often-quoted claim that roughly 87% of merchants use apps has similar provenance. Treat both as plausible folklore.

What practitioners can verify from working with stores directly: the distribution is wide and bimodal. Hobby stores run zero to two apps; established brands commonly run fifteen to thirty, accumulated over years and rarely audited. The under-discussed cost is not the subscriptions but the performance and data footprint, since every storefront-injected script slows pages and every installed app holds API access. If you take one action from this section, audit your installed apps quarterly, check what each one injects into your theme, and remove anything that cannot justify its fee in a sentence.

## What the numbers add up to

For **developers**: distribution is the constraint, not construction. The store adds over two thousand apps a month, categories near the money are saturated, and review inflation has neutralized ratings as a differentiator. The durable edges are niche selection, certification signals, and problems deep enough that an AI-generated weekend clone cannot follow you; the [app development guide](/blog/shopify-api-app-development-guide) covers the technical ground floor.

For **merchants**: the ecosystem's growth is mostly good news, more competition in every category and free tiers everywhere, but curation has shifted onto you. Fewer, better apps beats more apps, and the badge plus recent negative reviews are the fastest quality filter available.

## Next step

If you cite App Store numbers in your own work, pull them fresh: the trackers linked above update monthly, and this market moves fast enough that a six-month-old count is materially wrong. Merchants should spend the next thirty minutes on an app audit instead; the fastest ROI in this entire dataset is deleting an app you forgot you were paying for.
